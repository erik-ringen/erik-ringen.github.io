I am an evolutionary anthropologist
and a data scientist. Not always in that order.

Currently, I am wrapping up my PhD at Emory University where I develop novel quantitative methods to understand cross-cultural variation in human behavior and development. I am broadly-trained as a social and behavioral scientist, and have graduate-level training in Bayesian Statistics, Machine Learning, and Game Theory.

Before coming to Emory I studied Anthropology and Psychology at WSU Vancouver and worked as a Research Assistant for the Human Relations Area Files at Yale University.

RESEARCH

Recent Highlights

Meta-analysis of foraging returns

Pretelli, Ringen, & Lew-Levy (in prep)

Characterizing the development of children's foraging skill in 28 societies.

I developed a computational workflow in R to extract & clean data from studies. Stan model deals with missing data, measurement error, and other messiness.

Novel causal inference methods

Ringen, Martin, & Jaeggi (under revision)

Resource-use intensification can explain the emergence of large, sedentary societies with high degrees of inequality and hierarchy

I introduce a phylogenetic method for causal inference that generalizes beyond two binary variables, lifting a major constraint on comparative research.

Cross-cultural study of subsistence learning

Lew-Levy et al. (2021)

Ontogeny, learning strategies, and sex-biased teaching in two foraging societies

Bayesian multilevel model of learning that simultaneously models knowledge, transmission pathways, and perceived difficulty of tasks.

New article in Nature Sustainability

Ember et al. (2020)

Diversification provides resilience to societies facing environmental unpredictability and resource stress.

I analyzed records from 91 societies around the world using a multi-response, multilevel model that accounts for non-independence of observations.
